# Comet Croft — Theme

*A homestead under a strange sky.*

This doc describes the feel and intended experience of the pack, with no
regard for which mods exist or what's portable to our Minecraft version.
It's the north star; mod selection docs answer to this, not the other way
around.

## The one-sentence pitch

You tend a small, grounded homestead through turning seasons, while the sky
above you is alive — and every so often it reaches down and touches your
world.

## The two layers

The whole identity comes from the tension between two layers:

**The ground is cozy, slow, and yours.** Farming, cooking, animals,
building out a croft from a shack into a home. Progress here is measured in
harvests and rooms added, not boss kills. Nothing on the ground is urgent.
The base game's loop — punch tree, build house, grow food — is kept intact
and deepened, never replaced.

**The sky is vast, indifferent, and beautiful.** Richer nights: distinct
moons and moon phases, drifting comets, meteor showers, eclipses,
constellations that actually move across the year. You don't control the
sky. You learn to read it.

The player's emotional loop is stepping out of the warm layer into the cold
one: you finish a day's work, and then you look up.

## Time is the engine

Seasons and the celestial calendar are the same system viewed from two
angles — an almanac, not two features.

- **Seasons change what the ground asks of you.** Crops have growing
  seasons; winter is for indoor projects, preservation, and planning;
  spring is frantic planting. The year should have a *texture*, so that
  "it's autumn" means something about what you'll be doing today.
- **The sky keeps the long calendar.** Constellations mark the season;
  certain events only happen on certain nights of the year. A player who
  has lived a few in-game years should be able to glance at the night sky
  and know roughly where in the year they are, without a HUD.
- **Nothing is on a failure timer.** Missing an event or a planting window
  is a "catch it next year", never a punishment. The calendar creates
  anticipation, not anxiety.

## Celestial events reach the ground

This is the unique mechanic: the sky periodically intersects the croft.
Ideal behaviors, unconstrained:

- **Meteor falls.** During a shower, some streaks actually land — a small
  smoking crater somewhere nearby with sky-metal to mine. Rare enough that
  running out into the night with a torch feels like an occasion.
- **The passing comet.** Once in a long while (in-game years apart), a
  comet dominates the sky for a stretch of nights. While it hangs there,
  the world is subtly charged: crops shimmer and grow eagerly, animals act
  strange, traders carry oddities, unusual things bloom. Then it leaves,
  and the world exhales. Long-term players should tell time in comets —
  "I built the mill the year of the second comet."
- **Eclipses and rare nights.** A blood moon, a sun-dimming eclipse, an
  aurora — each a one-night mood shift with one small mechanical fingerprint
  (mobs bolder, certain plants harvestable only then), never a siege event.
- **Star-reading as progression.** The closest thing to a tech tree: a
  telescope, star charts, an almanac you fill in. Knowledge is the reward —
  predicting the next shower, charting when the comet returns. Mastery
  means *knowing the sky*, not owning better gear.

## What it must never become

- **Not a grind or a quest pack.** No questbook-driven progression. The
  almanac invites; it never assigns.
- **Not a magic pack.** Sky-metal makes tools and lanterns and a few
  wonders, not spellbooks. The sky stays astronomical, not arcane.
- **Not an event treadmill.** Rare means rare. If something spectacular
  happens every night, nothing is spectacular. Most nights are just quiet
  and starry — that's the baseline that makes event nights land.
- **Not a difficulty overhaul.** Combat, hunger, and death stay vanilla in
  spirit. Threat is atmosphere (a bolder blood-moon night), not a wall.

## Tone and aesthetics

- **Palette:** warm interiors, cold exteriors. Lantern-glow orange against
  deep night blues. Screenshots should look like a lit farmhouse window
  under an enormous sky.
- **Sound:** quiet is a feature. Ambient nature and weather on the ground;
  near-silence with rare, subtle tones for the sky. Event nights may have
  their own distant music.
- **UI:** the branding (menus, loading screens) sells the theme — night
  sky, a comet, a small farmhouse silhouette. In-game HUD stays vanilla-
  clean; the almanac/star-chart is the one bespoke interface, and it should
  feel like a hand-kept journal, not a dashboard.
- **Pace:** sessions should end with "one more night" because you want to
  see the sky, not because a bar is almost full.

## Litmus tests for any future addition

1. Does it deepen the ground layer or enrich the sky layer? If neither,
   it's QoL infrastructure or it's out.
2. Could a vanilla player pick this up and just play? (Vanilla++ promise.)
3. Does it respect the calendar — slotting into seasons/events rather than
   ignoring them?
4. Does it add urgency or anxiety? If yes, soften it or cut it.
